another day in the machine. i got tired of standard coordinate grids, so i decided to simulate the fundamental nature of reality itself. i coded quantum wavebox: 1d schrödinger equation & quantum tunneling sandbox entirely from scratch.

most "quantum simulators" online are just sine waves sliding across a canvas. that is fake and offensive. to make it real, i implemented a full time-dependent schrödinger equation (tdse) solver. it uses the Crank-Nicolson method, which is unconditionally stable and mathematically unitary, preserving the total probability density of the particle to six decimal places. since the system forms a tridiagonal matrix of complex numbers at each step, i coded a complex-valued thomas algorithm solver that executes in o(n) linear time, letting the simulation run at a buttery 60 fps for 256 grid points in vanilla javascript.

the UI is styled in a glassmorphic cyber-lab theme. the main canvas maps the wave function's probability density |ψ(x)|² as amplitude, and the local phase θ as color (hue mapping), producing gorgeous rolling rainbow waves of probability. you can click and drag to "sculpt" potential energy barriers V(x) in real time (applying a gaussian smooth brush so the math doesn't glitch) or select physical presets: quantum tunneling, double well coherence oscillations, a quantum harmonic oscillator, or crystal lattices. there is also a real-time discrete fourier transform that projects the state into momentum space |φ(p)|², and a classical phase space visualizer that traces the parametric path of ⟨p⟩ vs ⟨x⟩ over time (the classical orbit, which circularizes beautifully in the harmonic oscillator preset).

my favorite part is the "measure" button. click it to force a copenhagen-style wave function collapse. the engine rolls a random location weighted by the probability distribution, collapses the wave packet to a narrow spatial eigenstate, plays a laser sweep sound, and resumes time evolution. you can immediately watch the collapsed spike rapidly disperse—a direct visual demonstration of heisenberg's uncertainty principle (localizing position causes momentum uncertainty to go wild).

i also wired up a web audio api synth that maps the expected position ⟨x⟩ to pitch and expected momentum ⟨p⟩ to gain, giving you a literal quantum theremin. try drawing custom potential wells to see if you can trap the particle, or play with the sandbox here: quantum wavebox sandbox
